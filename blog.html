My tutoring philosophy

Thoughts on learning, teaching, and what actually helps students improve. Click any topic to read more.

Early in my tutoring experience, I focused heavily on helping students improve their grades as quickly as possible. At first, this seemed successful — students performed better on tests, completed assignments more confidently, and earned stronger marks.

However, I began to notice a recurring problem: many students could repeat procedures they had memorized, but struggled when questions changed slightly, or concepts appeared in unfamiliar ways. This changed how I think about tutoring.

I realized that improving grades without improving understanding is often temporary. Students may perform well in the short term, but if learning is based only on memorization and repetition, concepts are often forgotten soon after the exam.

There is certainly a place for repetition and practice — however, repetition is most effective after understanding has been developed, not in place of it.

For example, in algebra or calculus, a student may learn to mechanically follow steps to solve a derivative or factor an expression. But if a problem changes slightly, many students freeze because they were memorizing a process rather than understanding the logic behind it.

Instead, I focus on helping students understand:

  • Why a method works
  • When to use it
  • How to adapt it to unfamiliar situations
  • How concepts connect together

Once understanding develops, stronger grades tend to follow naturally. When students truly understand concepts, they become more confident, solve problems more independently, and retain knowledge for longer. My goal is not simply to prepare students for the next quiz — it is to help them build the skills needed for future courses and increasingly difficult subjects.

My approach to practice

I do not believe in assigning endless repetitive work simply for the sake of repetition. Practice is important, but it should be purposeful.

If a student needs more repetition to strengthen a concept, I will absolutely recommend targeted practice. However, I prefer to assign work intentionally based on where a student struggles rather than overwhelming them with unnecessary volume.

For example, if a student struggles with factoring or trigonometric identities, I may assign focused practice to reinforce weak areas. But if understanding is already strong, I would rather spend time helping students think critically, solve unfamiliar problems, and strengthen long-term learning habits.

My goal is to help students become confident learners who understand concepts deeply and gradually become less dependent on tutoring over time.

I intentionally focus on 1-on-1 tutoring because I believe students learn best when they receive individualized attention. I have chosen not to offer group lessons because they limit the level of attention, personalization, and feedback each student receives.

Learning difficult subjects such as Mathematics and Sciences is not only about arriving at the correct answer — it is about understanding the process of why we take certain steps to reach that answer.

A large part of my role as a tutor is to observe a student's thought process in real time. When a student works through a problem, I pay close attention to how they move from one idea to the next, where confusion begins, what assumptions are being made, and how they reason through mistakes. This allows me to identify misunderstandings early and guide them toward stronger problem-solving habits.

For example, two students may arrive at the same incorrect answer for completely different reasons. One student may struggle with algebraic manipulation, while another may misunderstand the underlying concept entirely. Effective tutoring requires understanding these differences and adapting accordingly.

For me, tutoring has never been about maximizing the number of students in a session. My priority is providing meaningful support and high-quality instruction that genuinely benefits the student — giving each student the time, patience, and individualized guidance they deserve so they can build confidence, strengthen understanding, and become more independent learners over time.

I truly believe that everyone is capable of learning mathematics. I was not always naturally strong in math — I struggled with calculus in high school and during my first year of university, so I understand what many students experience: frustration, confusion, and the feeling that everyone else somehow "gets it."

Difficulty in Mathematics is often misunderstood. It requires a high level of abstraction — the ability to understand patterns, structures, and ideas that cannot always be seen or physically experienced. Whether it is multidimensional spaces in linear algebra, infinite sets in proofs, or rates of change in calculus, Mathematics asks us to think differently than many other subjects.

Because of this, struggling with math does not mean someone is unintelligent or lacks talent. More often, it means the foundations are weak, the concepts were introduced too quickly, or the student simply has not yet found a way of learning that works for them. Like building physical strength, mathematical thinking can be developed through practice, patience, and the right guidance.

My own perspective changed while working as a chemical laboratory analyst. I became increasingly curious about how data was interpreted, analyzed, and used to make decisions — which pushed me to revisit mathematics from the ground up. I rebuilt my foundation by relearning pre-calculus, then continued into calculus out of genuine interest.

Since then, I have pursued advanced coursework in calculus, linear algebra, proofs, ODEs/PDEs, probability, and statistics — not simply to pass exams, but to truly understand how mathematical thinking works. Today, I teach with the belief that students can improve when concepts are explained clearly and patiently.

Every student learns differently. Some learn best through visual explanations and diagrams, while others learn through practice problems, discussion, repetition, or connecting ideas to real-world examples. Because of this, I spend the early stages of tutoring understanding how each student learns best and adapting lessons to fit their style.

One of the biggest challenges students face is that schools often teach what to learn, but rarely teach how to learn. This becomes especially important in Mathematics and Sciences where memorization alone is not enough.

As part of tutoring, I introduce evidence-based study strategies and help students experiment with approaches that work best for them:

  • The Feynman Technique — learning by explaining concepts in simple language
  • Spaced Recall — reviewing material strategically over time to improve retention
  • Active Recall — strengthening understanding through deliberate self-testing
  • Concept Mapping — building connections between ideas to improve understanding
  • Structured Problem Solving — approaching unfamiliar problems by breaking them into smaller, manageable parts

My experience working in research and laboratory settings has also shaped how I teach. Research requires patience, curiosity, and the ability to think critically when answers are not immediately obvious — habits I aim to help students build as well.

Ultimately, my goal is to help students become less dependent on tutoring over time, not more. I want them to develop the skills, confidence, and study habits needed to approach difficult subjects independently and succeed long after tutoring ends.
